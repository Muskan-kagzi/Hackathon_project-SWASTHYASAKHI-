Detect Early, Act Fast

Self-exam guides, early detection tools, and awareness to save lives.

Why Early Detection Matters

95% Survival Rate

When detected early, breast cancer has a 95% five-year survival rate. Early detection saves lives.

Know the Signs

Unusual lumps, changes in breast shape, or abnormal discharge are key warning signs to watch for.

Risk Factors

Age, family history, and lifestyle factors can increase risk. Regular screening is essential for everyone.

Self-Examination Guide

Breast Self-Examination

1

Look in Mirror

Check for changes in size, shape, or skin texture

2

Arms Raised

Lift arms overhead and look for dimpling or changes

3

Lying Down

Use flat fingers to feel for lumps in circular motions

4

In Shower

Check with soapy hands for easier movement

Cervical Health Awareness

Pap Smear Test

Regular Pap smears detect abnormal cervical cells early. Recommended every 3 years for women 21-65.

HPV Vaccine

HPV vaccination prevents most cervical cancers. Best when given before first sexual activity.

Screening Reminders

Monthly Self-Exam

Perform breast self-examination every month after your period.

Annual Mammogram

Schedule yearly mammograms if you're 40+ or have family history.

Pap Smear

Get a Pap smear every 3 years (ages 21-65) to screen for cervical cancer.

Busting Myths & Common Questions

Fact: 80% of women diagnosed with breast cancer have no family history. While family history increases risk, anyone can develop breast cancer.
Fact: Cervical cancer can occur at any age after puberty. Regular screening from age 21 is crucial for early detection.
Fact: Breast size has no correlation with cancer risk. All women, regardless of breast size, should perform regular self-exams.
Fact: There's a slight increase in cervical cancer risk with long-term oral contraceptive use, but the benefits usually outweigh the risks. Regular screening is key.
